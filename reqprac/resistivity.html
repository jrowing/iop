Determination of the Resistivity of a Metal Wire

by Joe Rowing
last updated 1/06/2018

Theory:

Resistivity, $œÅ$ can be found using the equation: $$R = \frac {\rho l}{A} $$ where $l$ is the length of the wire,
$A$ the cross-sectional area and
$R$ the resistance.
This can be compared with the equation for a straight line $y= mx + c $
A graph plotted of $R$ (y-axis) against $l$ (x-axis) will be a straight line through the origin of gradient $\frac {\rho}{A}$.
The cross sectional area can be found using $A = \pi r^{2}$ and the resistivity calculated by $\rho = \text{ gradient } \times A$.

Apparatus:

  • Meter rule +/- 1mm
  • Powersupply
  • Ammeter
  • Micrometer +/- 0.01mm
  • 110cm length of Nichrome wire
  • Voltmeter

Experimental Method:

The apparatus should be set up as shown.

Leaving one crocodile clip fixed at one end of the wire, the other clip should be moved along at suitable intervals, e.g. every 10 cm/20 cm to cover the whole range of the wire.
Readings on the voltmeter and ammeter should be noted for each length and the resistance determined using $R = \frac{V}{l}$. The diameter of the wire can be found using a micrometer or vernier calipers and the cross-sectional area determined. Plot a graph of R (y-axis) against l (x-axis) and calculate the resistivity using: $ \rho = \text{ gradient } \times A$.

Extension

By comparing the resistivity value obtained to known constants, it is possible to determine the type of metal making up different wires. Remember to quote your sources!
