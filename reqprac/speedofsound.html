Determination of the Speed of Sound Using Stationary Waves

by Joe Rowing
last updated 1/06/2018

Theory:

When resonance first occurs, the length of air in the tube, $l$, plus a small end correction, $e$ (to account for the position of the tuning fork or speaker above the tube) will be equal to a quarter of a wavelength. Hence: $$ l+ e = \frac{\lambda}{4} \text { and } \lambda = \frac{c}{f}$$ So $$l = \frac{c}{4f-e}$$ If a graph is plotted of $l$ (y-axis) against $1/f$ (x-axis) it should be a straight line with a small negative y-intercept. The gradient of the graph equals $c/4$ , and so the speed of sound, c, can be found. The small negative intercept will give the end correction.

Apparatus:

  • Speaker
  • Signal generator
  • Resonance tube
  • Water

Experimental Method:

The apparatus should be set up as shown.

Initially fill the resonance tube to the top with water. Slowly allow the water to drain. As this is being done hold the speaker over the top. When resonance occurs (a loud sound will be heard), measure the length of the tube above the water level. Repeat the above for a range of frequencies. Plot a graph of length (y-axis) against 1/frequency (x-axis). Use the gradient to determine a value for the speed of sound.
